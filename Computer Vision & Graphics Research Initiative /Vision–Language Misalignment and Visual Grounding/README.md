Vision–Language Models (VLMs) have become widely used for image–text matching, retrieval, and zero-shot recognition. Despite their strong performance, these models often exhibit vision–language misalignment, where the generated or selected language does not accurately reflect the visual content of the image. Such misalignment can arise from sensitivity to small visual changes, over-reliance on background context, or ambiguity in visual cues, leading to unstable or incorrect interpretations.

In this research, we study vision–language misalignment through controlled computer vision experiments by applying classical visual transformations such as cropping, occlusion, blurring, and color changes. We further explore lightweight CV-based grounding strategies, including region-based aggregation and foreground emphasis, to reduce misalignment without retraining the model. By evaluating alignment behavior before and after these interventions, the project aims to understand failure modes of VLMs and assess whether classical vision techniques can improve robustness and visual grounding.
